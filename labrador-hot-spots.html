Labrador Hot Spots: Causes, Treatment, and How to Prevent Them

Hot spots can grow from a small red patch to a weeping, painful lesion within hours. Labs are especially prone — here's what to do when you spot one and how to stop them from coming back.

Home›Blog›Labrador Hot Spots: Causes, Treatment, and How to Prevent Them

By Labrador Retriever Care Editorial Team  |  Updated May 19, 2026  |  Skin Health

If you own a Labrador who loves water, summer often comes with an unwelcome side effect: hot spots. Formally called acute moist dermatitis, these painful, red, oozing skin lesions appear suddenly and spread fast. A small irritated patch in the morning can become a palm-sized wound by afternoon. Labs are among the breeds most frequently affected, and for a clear anatomical reason.

This guide explains why Labs get hot spots so readily, how to recognize one early, what to do about it (and what not to), and the practical prevention steps that actually reduce recurrence.

What Is a Hot Spot and Why Do Labs Get Them?

A hot spot is a localized area of bacterial skin infection that develops and spreads rapidly when conditions are right. The skin becomes inflamed, moist, and warm — hence the name. Bacteria (typically Staphylococcus pseudintermedius or Pseudomonas) exploit a break in the skin barrier or multiply in trapped moisture, causing the surrounding tissue to break down.

The Labrador Coat Problem

Labs have a dense, double-layered, water-resistant coat — a feature that makes them excellent retrievers but also outstanding moisture traps. When a Lab swims, gets rained on, or is incompletely dried after a bath, water can penetrate the top coat and sit against the undercoat and skin for hours. This warm, wet microenvironment is exactly what bacteria need to multiply. Labs who swim daily in summer and are not dried thoroughly afterward are at the highest risk.

The Itch-Scratch-Lick Cycle

Hot spots almost always begin with some form of skin irritation that causes the dog to scratch, lick, or chew. This self-trauma breaks the skin, allowing bacteria to establish rapidly. Common initiating triggers include flea bites, insect stings, allergies, ear infections (dogs scratch their neck), and anal gland discomfort (dogs chew at their rump). Once the cycle starts — itch → scratch → broken skin → infection → more itch — it accelerates without intervention.

Why Summer Is Hot Spot Season

Heat and humidity accelerate bacterial growth. Dogs spend more time in water. Fleas are most active. Pollen counts peak, triggering allergy-related scratching. All of these converge in summer months to make June through September the peak hot spot season for Labs.

How to Recognize a Hot Spot Early

Early recognition dramatically improves outcomes. A hot spot caught in the first 1–2 hours is far easier to treat than one that has spread and become deeply infected overnight.

Visual Signs

Look for a clearly defined red, moist, sometimes hairless area on the skin surface. The lesion may have a wet, glistening appearance, crusty edges, or oozing discharge. The hair around it may be matted and damp. The dog will typically be paying intense attention to the area — licking, scratching, or biting at it repeatedly.

Behavioral Signs

  • Sudden, intense licking or chewing at one body area
  • Whimpering or yelping when you touch the area
  • Reluctance to be groomed near the affected spot
  • Visible agitation or restlessness
  • Rubbing the area along the ground or furniture

Common Locations on a Labrador

  • Neck and cheeks: Often linked to ear infections — the dog scratches its ear and damages the nearby skin
  • Rump and base of tail: Flea bite hypersensitivity or anal gland issues
  • Hip and thigh: Moisture accumulation after swimming
  • Side of the face: Trauma from rubbing against objects

Early detection tip: After every swim or bath, run your hands through your Lab's entire coat looking for any warm, moist, or matted patches. Catching trapped moisture before a hot spot forms is the ideal intervention.

How to Treat a Lab Hot Spot at Home

Mild, early-stage hot spots can be managed at home if caught within the first few hours and if the dog's behavior (not eating, extreme pain, fever) does not suggest a more serious infection. Follow these steps precisely — the order matters.

Step 1: Clip the Hair

Using electric clippers (not scissors), clip the hair over the hot spot and at least 2–3 centimetres beyond its visible edge. This step is non-negotiable: hair traps moisture against the wound and creates a sealed anaerobic environment where bacteria thrive. Clipping exposes the lesion to air. Use a blade guard to avoid cutting the skin, which may already be fragile.

Step 2: Clean Gently

Gently clean the area with a 2% chlorhexidine solution (not hydrogen peroxide or alcohol — both damage skin tissue and delay healing). Use a soft cloth or gauze pad with light dabbing motion. Do not scrub. Allow the area to dry completely — you can gently fan it or use a cool setting on a hair dryer held at arm's length.

Step 3: Apply Topical Treatment If Needed

For a small, mild lesion, leaving it clean and exposed to air is often sufficient. For a more inflamed lesion, a veterinary-approved topical antibacterial/steroid spray (available over the counter) can reduce inflammation and fight surface bacteria. Do not use human antibiotic creams — many are toxic if ingested, and Labs will lick.

Step 4: Prevent Licking Immediately

Fit an e-collar (cone) immediately and keep it on continuously until the hot spot has fully dried and scabbed. This is the step most owners skip, and it is the most important. A Lab that licks a hot spot can double its size in an hour. Inflatable collars provide more comfort than rigid plastic ones; soft fabric cones are less effective at preventing determined lickers.

Step 5: Monitor for 48 Hours

Check the lesion twice daily. Signs of improvement: the area is drying, redness is fading, no new expansion. Signs that require veterinary attention: the lesion is growing, has a foul smell, feels hot and firm (deep infection), or the dog shows signs of systemic illness (lethargy, fever, loss of appetite).

When to See a Vet

Home treatment has its limits. See a veterinarian promptly if:

  • The hot spot is larger than your palm
  • It has a foul, pungent odour (deep tissue infection)
  • The area feels firm, warm, and swollen beyond the surface
  • Your Lab is clearly in significant pain
  • There is no improvement after 48 hours of home care
  • The dog shows signs of illness: lethargy, fever, or not eating
  • Multiple hot spots appear simultaneously

Veterinary treatment typically involves oral antibiotics (usually a 2–3 week course), a short course of oral steroids to break the itch cycle, and possibly a prescription topical spray. The vet will also try to identify and address the underlying trigger to prevent recurrence.

Hot Spot Prevention for Labradors

The single most impactful prevention step for a water-loving Lab is thorough drying after every water exposure. This alone addresses the most common trigger for the breed.

Drying Protocol After Swimming

Towel dry vigorously — work against the grain of the coat to reach the undercoat. Follow with a high-velocity dryer (a forced-air pet dryer) if available. Pay extra attention to the neck, collar area, armpits, and groin — areas where water pools and the coat is thickest. Remove the collar while drying and replace only when the neck is fully dry. Never let a wet Lab go to bed without drying — an overnight of trapped moisture is a common hot spot origin story.

Flea and Parasite Control

Flea allergy dermatitis is the most common cause of rump hot spots in Labs. A single flea bite can trigger a reaction that leads to days of scratching and a significant hot spot. Year-round flea prevention — not just summer treatment — is the standard of care for Labs in moderate to high flea-pressure environments.

Regular Grooming and Coat Inspection

Brushing 2–3 times per week does more than just control Lab shedding — it allows you to feel for any warm, moist, or matted patches before they develop into full hot spots. Run your hands along the entire body, including under the collar, behind the ears, and along the belly. Early detection is the easiest intervention.

Addressing Underlying Allergies

If your Lab gets hot spots repeatedly despite good drying and parasite control, allergies are the likely underlying cause. Discuss a food elimination trial or allergy testing with your vet. Switching to a novel protein food for 8–12 weeks can determine if food is a factor. Environmental allergies may require antihistamines, omega-3 supplementation, or in persistent cases, immunotherapy.

Labs at Risk for Bloat

Dogs with chronic skin discomfort sometimes show behavioral changes — disrupted sleep, anxiety around touch — that affect their overall routine. Labs managing recurring skin issues can also develop the kind of stress-related behaviors associated with other Lab health risks, including changes in eating patterns. Frequently Asked Questions

What causes hot spots on Labradors?

Hot spots in Labs are most often triggered by moisture trapped in the dense double coat after swimming or bathing. The damp, warm environment allows bacteria to multiply rapidly. Allergies, flea bites, and self-trauma from scratching are other common triggers.

How fast do hot spots grow on Labs?

Hot spots can double or triple in size within hours. What looks like a small red patch at noon can be a palm-sized weeping lesion by evening. Catching a hot spot within the first few hours makes it dramatically easier to manage.

Can I treat my Lab's hot spot at home?

Small, early-stage hot spots can be managed at home: clip the hair, clean gently with diluted chlorhexidine, keep it dry, and prevent licking with a cone. However, if the hot spot is larger than a palm, deep, foul-smelling, or not improving within 48 hours, see a vet.

Where do hot spots most commonly appear on Labradors?

The most common locations are the neck and cheeks (from ear scratching), the rump and base of the tail (from flea bites), the hip area, and the side of the face. Any area where moisture accumulates or where the dog scratches frequently is at risk.

What should I NOT put on a dog's hot spot?

Never use human products on hot spots: no Neosporin, no hydrogen peroxide, no rubbing alcohol, no hydrocortisone cream, no Vaseline, no tea tree oil. Many human ointments encourage licking or seal the wound — hot spots need airflow to heal.

Are Labradors more prone to hot spots than other breeds?

Yes. Labs have a dense, water-resistant double coat that traps moisture close to the skin. Combined with their love of swimming and water, this creates ideal conditions for bacterial proliferation. Labs are consistently listed among the highest-risk breeds for acute moist dermatitis.

How long does it take for a hot spot to heal on a Lab?

With proper treatment, a mild hot spot can begin to dry and heal within 3–5 days. Moderate cases treated with antibiotics typically clear in 7–14 days. The lesion will scab, the scab will fall off, and hair will regrow over 4–6 weeks.

Can allergies cause hot spots in Labs?

Yes — allergies are a major underlying trigger. Food allergies and environmental allergies (pollen, mold, dust mites) cause itching, which leads the dog to scratch and chew, breaking the skin barrier. If your Lab gets recurring hot spots, allergy testing may be warranted.

Do I need to shave the area around a hot spot?

Yes — clipping the hair around the lesion is an important first step. Hair traps moisture and bacteria against the wound. Clip a margin of at least 1–2 cm beyond the visible edge. Use clippers, not scissors, to avoid cutting the skin.

How do I prevent hot spots from recurring in my Labrador?

Key prevention steps: thoroughly dry your Lab after every swim or bath, maintain year-round flea prevention, address underlying allergies, brush regularly to check for moisture or matting, and inspect the coat after any water activity.
